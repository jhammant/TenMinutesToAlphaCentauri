§1 — The distance

How far is 4.37 light-years, really?

Light is the fastest thing in the universe. It goes around Earth 7.5 times every second. From the Sun to Earth takes it about 8 minutes. To Pluto, 5.5 hours. And yet to reach Alpha Centauri — our literal next-door neighbour in the galactic suburbs — it takes light four years and four months.

Space is staggeringly empty. If the Sun were a basketball in London, Alpha Centauri would be another basketball… somewhere in Tokyo.

This is the scenario most science-fiction-adjacent engineers actually dream about. No exotic g-forces, no energy bills measured in civilisations. Just a very, very efficient engine that can sustain 1g for years. Humanity can't build one yet — but the physics is tame. 3.58 years of your life for a round trip to the nearest star: that's the deal Einstein puts on the table.

§4 — What it would feel like

Out the window.

Near light-speed, the universe stops looking like anything you've ever seen. The starfield compresses forward into a brilliant ring around your direction of travel. Stars behind you fade and redden. Stars ahead shift into UV. Use the dial — the same one as above — to look through the cockpit.

At 0.5 c: stars noticeably shift forward. Nothing too alarming yet — your coffee isn't even spilling.
🕰️

Your watch runs slow

Time literally passes more slowly for you. Not an illusion — the universe is running two clocks, and they're no longer in sync.

📐

Space squashes

From your ship's frame, the 4.37 light-year gap shrinks. At γ=230,000, it's about 55 light-seconds wide — so of course you cross it in 10 minutes.

🌈

The sky turns into a tunnel

Relativistic aberration bunches all the starlight into a bright forward ring. Behind you: black. Ahead: a pinprick of compressed galaxy.

💀

Everyone you knew is gone

You come home to an Earth 8+ years older — or centuries, depending on how hard you pushed the dial. The twin paradox isn't a paradox. It's a bill.

§5 — The 10-minute trip

So. Ten minutes?

Yes. If your ship can hit a Lorentz factor of roughly γ ≈ 230,000 — a velocity so close to c you'd need scientific notation to count the nines — then the entire 4.37-light-year crossing lasts about 600 seconds on your watch.

The universe doesn't care how long your trip took from Earth's point of view. It gives you the clock that comes with your motion. And that clock can tick very, very slowly.

— Adapted from Gregory Gbur's lovely explainer "Relativity: Ten Minutes to Alpha Centauri" (Skulls in the Stars, 2012).

There's a catch, of course. Two, actually. (1) Nobody on Earth has any idea how to accelerate a spaceship — let alone a human — to γ = 230,000. The energy required approaches silly-physics territory: more than humanity's total annual energy output, for a single kilogram. (2) Even a modest 1g acceleration — which is Earth-gravity-comfortable — would, over months, get you there in about 3.5 years of ship-time. Accelerating hard enough to compress the trip into 10 minutes would paste you against the back wall at millions of g's. Bring a trampoline.

But the physics? The physics permits it. The universe is fine with a 10-minute trip to Alpha Centauri. It just wants you to pay the ticket price — in nines.
